## MoQ for the Fans
First, I wanted to address the SUV in the room.
My previous blog post, [You Don't Need It](https://moq.dev/blog/you-dont-need-it/), mentioned that you don't need MoQ for high-quality content.
It's true, but *as always*, there are exceptions to my shitty opinions.

But before we disprove one shitty opinion, we need to prove another.
**Racing is boring**.

The video footage is **so boring** that the only thing separating two laps is a counter.
Like yes, sometimes the car goes boom, but there's so much dead air.
I postulate that you're watching to be part of the story **LIVE**, not to see every millisecond of lap 392 in the Toilet Bowl 500.

And might actually a good use-case for MoQ.
Because instead of buffering/pausing to load boring content, we can instead skip it to keep latency low.
It keeps you in the action with **sub-second latency**.
And milliseconds might be the difference between winning and losing.

**FUN FACT**: Sports ~betting~ predication markets are a thing.

But maybe you're an oddball and want to watch the entire race.
Fine that's like your opinion, man.
Another benefit of MoQ are **multiple camera feeds**:

-   the main feed
-   the pit lane
- the live polymarket odds
- the other joke here

You don't want the selected stream buffering while the other streams are flawless.
Main stream good, side streams bad.

**Spoiler**:  MoQ lets you prioritize the main stream.

This works because every camera feed goes through the same QUIC connection.
It's one big priority queue, which QUIC uses to decide which packet to transmit next.
We're not subject to TCP head-of-line blocking like HTTP/2.

**FUN FACT**: HTTP/3 [only has 8 priority levels](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9218.html#name-urgency).
It's one of the reasons why MoQ uses WebTransport/QUIC directly instead, but you could build something similar to MoQ over HTTP/3.


## MoQ for the Cars
If you're a history buff, you'll know that I made MoQ originally [for the fans](/blog/distribution-at-twitch).

But people keep running my code on the cars.

And frankly, this is the better use-case for MoQ.

You might have pieced together that I don't watch racing (**boring**) outside of critically acclaimed movies for children.
Staring Lightning McQueen and his goofy comrades, the cars go fast vroom vroom.
But when I do see snippets of F1, the camera feeds from the cars are **awful**.

Me, the uninformed dumbass, has a hunch that it's due to networking.
See, there's going to be deadzones or interference or whatever around the track.
I'm a video star, not a radio star, so I just accept it as an unfixable problem.

**Boring protocols** like RTP drop packets during network events, which shows up as **artifacting** or **tearing** or **frozen video**.
The publisher gives up transmitting a frame after ~100 milliseconds; it's gone forever.

But **the chad protocol** doesn't give up.
**The chad protocol** lets each subscriber decide how long to wait.

MoQ publishers avoid dropping old data and instead prioritizes the newer stuff (in dependency order).
The old stuff gets queued in RAM (up to a TTL); it's basically free real-estate.
When bandwidth recovers, we can backfill old footage instead of losing it forever.

The **live stream** might give up after 1 second while the **instant relay** gives up after 10 seconds.
All that extra time means more time for old frames to arrive, which means higher production value.

The packets never gave up, just like Lightning McQueen never gives up.
They eventually make it to the finish line and the crowd goes **HONK HONK**.


## MoQ for the Robots
We're closer to the Cars Cinematic Universe than we want to admit.
Humans are a dying breed.
Like a pug on a hot day.
**RIP us.**

Enter the robots.
They byte.
And they need networking for their bleeps and bloops.
It turns out that MoQ works for more than just media:
- The speedometer? **A live stream**
- The steering wheel? **A live stream**
- The G force sensor as Lightning McQueen crashes into a wall? **A live stream**

Seriously, there's nothing special about media.
It's just delta encoded data.
You can delta encode a lot of things.
Just like I delta encoded your mom last night.

But networks aren't infinite.
We split up media into tracks/groups/frames/packets so pieces can be dropped when needed.
The hard part is that we need to put everything back together on the other end.

That's why we use timestamps baybee, they tell us when stuff happened relative to other stuff.
So do the same thing for metadata.
Stamp that fellow, using the same clock as media, and send it over the same pipe.

And you can take the red pill and prioritize **EVERYTHING**.
`controls > audio > metadata > video`
The QUIC connection is *cooperative* and will take care of the rest.

And yes, this sounds simple but it's something WebRTC completely botches.
You have to use a separate connection for metadata and timestamps aren't exposed in the browser.

**FUN FACT**: MoQ is already being used remotely to pilot drones over the internet.
One day we'll be able to remotely drive Lightning McQueen.


## MoQ for the Track
We're not done yet.
Sit back in your seat.
Fasten that seatbelt.
It's the law, and I'm the sheriff from the critically acclaimed movie Cars 2.

If more than one broadcast studio wants footage of Lightning McQueen's tailpipe, we don't want to transmit multiple copies.
Our satellite truck is expensive and has a limited bandwidth budget.

And what if none of the broadcast studios actually want that hot, smokey, tailpipe footage?
Do we even want to transmit it, or is it just a waste of bandwidth?

With all of this data flowing around the track, there has to be some way to organize it.
Enter stage right: **moq-relay**

It's a simple proxy that connects **1 publisher** with **N subscribers** (per broadcast/track).
When **moq-relay** instances connect to each other, they automatically form a cluster.
You can host this shit yourself [and/or pay me](https://pro.moq.dev).

It handles discovery, routing, and proxying. For example:
- **Lightning McQueen** connects to a self-hosted moq-relay at `192.168.420.69`.
- **moq-relay** is run with `--cluster-connect https://cdn.moq.dev/cars2`.
- **Studio clients** (or a relay!) connects to `https://cdn.moq.dev/cars2`.

Just like a tinder hookup, there's a match and now you can pub/sub.
Any tracks published by Lightning McQueen are automatically available to broadcast studio A, proxied though multiple relays.

And only one copy is ever transmitted between each **moq-relay** instance, regardless of the number of downstream subscribers.
This is known colloquially as "big deal" when flakey venue internet and expensive satellite trucks are involved.
Your self-hosted relay can funnel everything through a single outbound pipe!

And there's no limit to the number of connections you can establish.
[moq-lite](https://doc.moq.dev/concept/layer/moq-lite.html) includes the number of hops, so the subscriber uses the connection with the shortest path.
Go ahead, connect to `cdn.moq.dev` and `cloudflare.mediaoverquic.com` at the same time; they sit idle until someone subscribes.
Maybe one connection is over ethernet while the other is over satellite; **go wild you honker**.

It's like swiping right on Lightning McQueen multiple times on alt accounts.
Don't judge.
